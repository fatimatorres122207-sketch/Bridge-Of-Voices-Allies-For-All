Being first-generation means stepping into every space with dual responsibility — honoring where I came from while simultaneously building something that has never existed before me. I moved to the United States from Mexico at six years old, long before I understood the weight of that transition. As a Mexican first-generation woman, I grew up learning to navigate systems, expectations, and opportunities without a roadmap. There was no older sibling to ask, no parent who had gone through college here, no example to copy. Every choice felt like I was carving the path from scratch — academically, socially, financially, and professionally. It is not easy being first-generation. I faced challenges while also helping my parents navigate this country. I began translating for my family at a young age — in hospitals, at parent-teacher conferences, and even attempting to interpret legal documents. Those experiences force you to grow up fast. I always felt older than my age, and in many ways, I lost parts of my childhood to responsibility. When I went to college, I felt out of place. I didn’t have the privilege of parents who could guide me or an older sibling who had done it first. It was me. I had to learn everything the hard way. I was the first daughter, granddaughter, and cousin to go to college. Through trial and error, I figured it out — and I graduated. I made sure to pass down everything I learned to my younger cousins so they wouldn’t struggle the way I did. Seeing their transition into college become easier because of my guidance made every struggle worth it. These experiences shaped the woman I am today.
